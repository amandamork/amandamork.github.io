04 — Letter to DeepLearning.AI

On building the room developers want to be in.

Andrew has been on the TED AI stage. What stuck with me wasn't the talk. It was the way he stayed afterward, treating the conversations with developers as the actual product. That instinct is what AI Dev needs. Conferences for builders only work when the room belongs to the builders, and the people running the program understand the difference between a stage and a community.

That's the work I've been doing at TED AI. I built the event line from scratch in six months. A sold-out flagship at $1M+ ticket revenue. A monthly salon series with 50% net-new sponsor participation. Builder working sessions and a 17,211-contact audience database driving 850K+ touchpoints a year with zero paid media. The program ranks #1 in engagement per post on LinkedIn against a16z TechWeek with 2.4x fewer followers, and runs a 500-person speaker alumni flywheel that compounds every cycle.

Before TED AI, I scaled TEDxSanFrancisco from hundreds of attendees to 15,000, built a $500K annual sponsorship engine, and learned the lesson that shapes how I work now: the best event lines aren't built on big budgets. They're built on repeatable systems that turn one-time attendees into people who refer their friends, return as sponsors, and bring their employer with them.

AI Dev sits at the moment where it can either become another AI conference or become the developer-first event brand the field is missing. I want to be the person who decides which. I bring an active speaker network across the labs and founding teams, a working playbook for revenue-generating event programs, and the operational instincts to build a team and a business line at the same time.

I'd be glad to talk through how this maps to what you're building.

— Amanda
